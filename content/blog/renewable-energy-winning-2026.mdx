For years, the conversation about renewable energy was framed around potential — what solar and wind *could* do once they scaled up, became cheaper, and solved the storage problem. That conversation has fundamentally changed.

Renewables are not the future of energy. **They are the present.** The data from 2025 and early 2026 makes the trajectory unmistakable, and the irony is that a geopolitical crisis — the US-Iran conflict and its impact on oil prices — is accelerating the shift in ways that policy alone never could.

## The Cost Story Nobody Can Argue With

The price of solar electricity has fallen by **approximately 90% over the past decade**. Wind has followed a similar trajectory. These are not forecasts or projections — they are recorded market prices from actual contracts.

In 2026, solar power is the cheapest source of new electricity generation in the majority of the world's major markets. That includes India, where solar now routinely undercuts coal in competitive tender processes. It includes Australia, the Middle East, the US Sun Belt, and significant parts of Europe.

**Battery storage costs have followed the same curve.** Lithium-ion battery prices have fallen from over $1,000 per kilowatt-hour in 2010 to under $90 in 2026. This price decline is what makes the "intermittency problem" — the fact that solar does not generate electricity at night, and wind does not blow on calm days — solvable at scale.

The economics have crossed a threshold that is hard to reverse. Once renewables are cheaper than fossil fuels for new generation, the logic for building new coal or gas plants largely disappears unless other factors intervene.

## The Deployment Numbers

Global renewable energy capacity additions hit a new record in 2025, with over **500 gigawatts** of new solar and wind capacity installed in a single year. To put that in context, 500 gigawatts is roughly the equivalent of the entire generating capacity of the United States from all sources combined, added in a single year.

China is the dominant force in global clean energy deployment — by a wide margin. It installed more solar capacity in 2025 than the rest of the world combined, and its domestic manufacturing base has driven down global panel prices to levels that make solar viable in markets that would have been considered non-starters five years ago.

**India is the most important growth story.** With a massive and growing electricity demand, a government committed to 500 gigawatts of renewable capacity by 2030, and a solar irradiation profile that makes it one of the best places on Earth for solar generation, India's clean energy expansion is reshaping global energy investment flows.

Europe, accelerated by the energy security shock of the Ukraine conflict in the early 2020s, has built out wind capacity at an unprecedented pace. Offshore wind in particular has become a major industry, with projects now operating in the North Sea, the Baltic, and increasingly in the Mediterranean.

## The Iran Effect: Fossil Fuel Volatility Drives Faster Transition

The current conflict in the Middle East is — counterintuitively — functioning as an accelerant for the energy transition.

Oil above $100 per barrel makes every alternative look more attractive. **Governments that were debating the pace of EV adoption** are now having more urgent conversations. **Industrial companies facing energy cost pressure** are accelerating decisions about on-site solar and efficiency investments. **Consumers in petrol-heavy markets** are looking at electric vehicles with fresh eyes as fuel bills rise.

This is not a new dynamic. Every major oil price shock in the past 50 years has temporarily boosted investment in alternatives. What is different in 2026 is that the alternatives are cost-competitive in a way they were not in 1973 or 2008. The transition does not need the price shock to make economic sense. The shock simply speeds up decisions that were already being made.

## What Still Needs to Happen

Acknowledging the genuine progress does not mean the transition is complete or that the hard problems are solved.

**Grid infrastructure** remains the most significant bottleneck. Generating more renewable electricity is one challenge. Transmitting it efficiently from where it is generated to where it is consumed requires transmission lines, grid upgrades, and smart management systems — investments that have lagged behind generation capacity growth in most markets.

**Industrial decarbonisation** — steel, cement, chemicals, aviation, shipping — is far harder than electricity decarbonisation and has progressed much more slowly. These sectors account for a significant portion of global emissions, and the technical and economic solutions are not yet at the same stage of development as solar and wind.

**The Global South** faces a different set of challenges. Access to capital for clean energy projects in lower-income countries is constrained by higher borrowing costs and lower credit ratings. The international finance mechanisms needed to bridge this gap exist in principle but remain inadequate in practice.

These are real challenges. They are also solvable challenges — and the pace at which the renewable industry has solved previous problems, from cost to reliability to storage, suggests they will not remain unsolved for long.

The energy transition is not a story of smooth, linear progress. It is messy, uneven, and interrupted by politics, economics, and conflict. But the underlying direction is clear, and in 2026, it is difficult to look at the data and reach any other conclusion: **renewable energy is winning**.

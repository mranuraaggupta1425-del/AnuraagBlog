Tax season has always been stressful. The forms are complex, the rules change every year, and professional accountants are expensive. So it is not surprising that when capable AI chatbots became widely available, a significant portion of the population turned to them for help.

What is surprising is how quickly adoption has grown. Roughly **1 in 4 Americans** used an AI chatbot to assist with their tax preparation this filing season — a figure that reflects both the genuine utility of these tools and a level of trust that financial professionals are urging people to treat with considerable caution.

## What AI Tools Are Actually Good at in Tax Preparation

To be fair to the technology, AI chatbots are genuinely useful for certain parts of the tax preparation process — and it helps to understand which parts before deciding how much to rely on them.

**Explaining concepts** is where these tools shine. Ask an AI to explain what a Schedule C is, how self-employment tax works, or what the difference between a tax credit and a tax deduction means, and you will typically get a clear, accurate, jargon-free explanation. This kind of financial education has historically been hard to access without paying for professional advice or sitting through dense IRS documentation.

**Walking through standard deductions** is another area where AI performs well. For straightforward situations — a salaried employee with W-2 income, standard deduction, and no complex assets — an AI can guide someone through the logical flow of a return reasonably reliably.

**Organising documents** is a third useful function. AI can help users build a checklist of what they need, identify which forms they should be expecting, and flag documents that might have been overlooked.

## Where the Problems Start

The concerns raised by tax professionals are real and specific — not just generic warnings about AI being imperfect.

**Outdated tax law** is the most significant risk. AI models are trained on data up to a certain cutoff date. Tax laws change every year — sometimes significantly. New deductions are added, old ones expire, income thresholds shift, contribution limits change. A model trained on 2024 data giving advice about a 2025 tax return may be applying rules that no longer exist in the same form.

**State taxes** are where errors compound. Federal tax rules are already complex; state tax codes are even more varied. Rules differ significantly across states, and AI systems are considerably less reliable when navigating state-level tax obligations compared to federal ones.

**Unusual situations** expose the limits of general-purpose AI sharply. If someone has rental income, stock option exercises, cryptocurrency transactions, a home office deduction, or foreign income, the number of edge cases and specific IRS rules that apply grows exponentially. These are exactly the situations where a wrong answer costs the most money.

**Hallucination** — the tendency of AI to produce confident-sounding but factually incorrect information — remains a genuine risk. Several documented cases have emerged this filing season of AI tools citing deductions, credits, or thresholds that do not exist in current tax law.

## What Financial Experts Actually Recommend

The consensus among accountants and financial planners is not "never use AI for taxes." It is more nuanced than that.

**Use AI to understand, not to file.** There is a meaningful difference between using an AI to help you understand what a form means or what category an expense falls into, versus using it to calculate and submit your return. The former is generally low-risk and genuinely useful. The latter carries real financial and legal exposure.

**Cross-reference everything.** If an AI tells you about a specific deduction or credit, verify it directly on IRS.gov or the relevant state revenue website before claiming it. The few minutes this takes can save significant money and stress.

**For complex returns, invest in a professional.** The cost of a qualified accountant is almost always justified for anyone with self-employment income, investments, rental properties, or major life changes in the tax year. The potential downside of an error — interest, penalties, or audit exposure — typically far exceeds the professional fee.

## The Broader Question: AI and Financial Decision-Making

The AI-and-taxes conversation is part of a larger and more important question about where AI fits into personal financial decision-making.

The technology is powerful. It is getting better rapidly. And it is making financial information accessible to people who previously had no affordable way to access it — which is genuinely valuable.

But financial decisions have real consequences. A misunderstood tax rule does not just produce a wrong answer in a conversation — it can result in an underpayment, a penalty, or an audit. The stakes are different from asking an AI to recommend a movie or explain a historical event.

The right mental model is to treat AI as a **knowledgeable starting point, not a final authority**. It can help you ask better questions, understand your situation more clearly, and prepare for a conversation with a professional. It cannot replace the professional judgment and accountability that a qualified adviser provides.

As AI capabilities continue to advance, this balance will shift. But for the 2026 filing season, the cautious approach is also the smart one.

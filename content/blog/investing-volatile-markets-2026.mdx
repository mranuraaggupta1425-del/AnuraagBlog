Market volatility is uncomfortable. Watching a portfolio fall in value — even temporarily — triggers a level of stress that academic finance theory tends to underestimate. The rational thing to do in a market downturn is often to do nothing. The human thing is to feel the urge to act. Understanding that gap, and managing it, is at the heart of successful long-term investing.

In 2026, that gap has been tested repeatedly. The US-Iran conflict has driven energy prices to multi-year highs. Interest rate expectations have shifted. Several major indices have given back their January gains. And the financial media — which profits from engagement, not from calm — has been relentlessly amplifying every negative signal.

Here is a grounded framework for thinking about money in this environment.

## The Single Most Important Variable: Your Time Horizon

Every investment decision should start with the same question: **when will I need this money?**

If the answer is more than ten years, the current market volatility is almost irrelevant to your final outcome. The historical record is clear: diversified equity portfolios over ten-year periods have produced positive real returns in the vast majority of cases, including periods that began at the peak of a market cycle.

If the answer is less than three years, money you genuinely need — for a house deposit, a child's education, a planned major expense — should not be in equities at all. **Short-term needs belong in cash or short-duration bonds**, regardless of what the market is doing. This is not a commentary on the market's direction; it is a statement about matching risk to purpose.

The middle ground — a three-to-ten-year horizon — is where the decisions are genuinely more complex, and where thoughtful asset allocation matters most.

## What the Current Market Is Actually Doing

Stripping away the noise, 2026 markets are navigating three main forces.

**Energy and inflation** are the dominant macro theme. Oil above $100 per barrel feeds into transportation costs, manufacturing inputs, and household energy bills. That inflationary pressure gives central banks less room to cut rates, which makes borrowing more expensive and puts pressure on growth-sensitive sectors.

**Geopolitical risk premium** is being priced into assets in ways that are visible but hard to quantify. Defence stocks have surged. Safe-haven assets — gold, the dollar, Swiss francs — have attracted flows. Emerging market currencies and equities have faced headwinds as the dollar strengthens.

**AI-driven earnings growth** is providing a partial offset. Companies with genuine AI revenue — not just AI marketing — have delivered strong earnings that have supported technology sector valuations even as the broader market has wobbled. This is not speculation about the future; it is companies reporting actual revenue from AI products.

These three forces are pulling in different directions, which is precisely why the market feels uncertain. It is not broken. It is processing genuinely conflicting signals.

## The Behaviours That Destroy Returns

The academic research on investor behaviour is consistent and sobering. The average investor earns significantly less than the average fund, because of the decisions they make around timing.

**Selling during downturns** locks in losses that would otherwise be temporary. Most investors who sold equities during the 2020 COVID crash, the 2022 rate shock, or the 2025 growth scare missed the subsequent recoveries — which, in each case, were significant.

**Chasing recent performance** leads people to buy what has already risen and sell what has already fallen — the opposite of what value-aware investing requires. The assets that felt safest at the peak of the 2021 technology bubble were among the worst performers over the following two years.

**Overreacting to news** conflates being informed with the need to act. Following the news closely is not the same as needing to change your portfolio every time a new development emerges. For long-term investors, most news events are noise rather than signal.

## A Practical Framework for Right Now

Given where markets are in March 2026, here are the principles that experienced investors are applying.

**Rebalance, do not retreat.** If equity markets have fallen and your allocation has shifted away from your target, this is a natural moment to rebalance — which means buying equities at lower prices, not selling them. Rebalancing is one of the few systematic behaviours that demonstrably adds value over time.

**Review sectors, not just indices.** The headline index number masks significant differences across sectors. Energy and defence have outperformed significantly. Consumer discretionary and growth-oriented technology have underperformed. Understanding where within the market you have exposure matters more than watching the headline number.

**Consider duration in fixed income.** With rate cut expectations being pushed out, longer-duration bonds carry more interest rate risk than shorter ones. Investors with significant fixed income allocations may benefit from reviewing whether they are comfortable with the duration exposure they currently hold.

**Do not ignore cash.** In an environment where short-term rates are still meaningfully positive, holding cash — in a high-yield savings account or a short-duration money market fund — is not a cop-out. It is a legitimate position that provides both income and optionality.

The market in 2026 is not the most difficult environment investors have faced. But it is one that rewards patience, clarity about goals, and the discipline to resist the constant pressure to do something. Sometimes the best investment decision is the one you do not make.

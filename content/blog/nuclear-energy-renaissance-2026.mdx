There's a particular kind of policy reversal that happens slowly, then all at once. Nuclear energy has been building toward one for years. But watching it actually arrive — with governments reversing phase-out decisions, tech giants signing multi-decade power purchase agreements with reactor operators, and a new generation of engineers entering the field with genuine excitement — still feels surprising.

It shouldn't. The logic was always there. What changed is the urgency.

![Cooling towers of a nuclear power plant at sunrise — clean power for a carbon-constrained world](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1509391366360-2e959784a276?w=1200&q=80)

## The Case That Wouldn't Go Away

For much of the 2010s, nuclear energy seemed to be losing the argument. Fukushima in 2011 triggered phase-out decisions across Europe. Germany accelerated its nuclear exit. Switzerland, Belgium, and Spain announced wind-downs. The economics appeared bleak: new large-scale plants in the West were running dramatically over budget and over time. Hinkley Point C in the UK and the Vogtle plants in the US became cautionary tales about the difficulty of building nuclear infrastructure in modern regulatory environments.

And yet, through all of that, the fundamental facts never changed. Nuclear is the highest-density, lowest-carbon, most reliable source of electricity that humans have ever built. A uranium fuel pellet the size of a fingertip contains roughly the same energy as 17,000 cubic feet of natural gas, 1,780 pounds of coal, or 149 gallons of oil. It generates power 24 hours a day, regardless of whether the wind is blowing or the sun is shining. For a world that needs to decarbonise its electricity supply while keeping the lights on, it is extraordinarily difficult to build a credible plan that doesn't include it.

The climate mathematics eventually reasserted themselves. And then the data centre boom made them impossible to ignore.

## The AI Electricity Problem

Here's a connection most people don't immediately make: the explosion in AI computing demand has become one of the most powerful drivers of the nuclear energy revival.

Training large AI models is extraordinarily energy-intensive. Running inference — actually using those models at scale — is even more demanding in aggregate. The hyperscalers — Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta — have made massive AI infrastructure commitments. And they've simultaneously made net-zero pledges that their investors and regulators are watching carefully.

Renewable energy, while impressive in its cost reductions, has a fundamental limitation: intermittency. You cannot guarantee that a data centre will have power on a calm night in winter using only solar and wind. You need either storage — which is improving but not yet at the required scale — or a dispatchable, always-on source of clean power. Nuclear is the only mature technology that fits that description.

![High-voltage power lines transmitting clean electricity from nuclear plant to cities](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1497435334941-8c899a9bd6b4?w=1200&q=80)

Microsoft's deal to restart a unit at Three Mile Island — yes, *that* Three Mile Island — sent a signal that no policy paper could. When one of the world's most valuable companies makes a 20-year commitment to nuclear power for its data centres, it changes the investment calculus for the entire industry. Google followed with its own agreements. Amazon has been buying nuclear capacity in multiple markets. The tech sector's appetite for reliable, clean baseload power is providing the long-term revenue certainty that nuclear economics require.

## Small Modular Reactors: Hype, Hope, or Both?

The term you'll encounter most in any conversation about nuclear's future is Small Modular Reactors — SMRs. The concept is straightforward: instead of the enormous, custom-engineered plants that take 15 years and billions of pounds to build, design a standardised reactor that is factory-manufactured, shipped in modules, and assembled on-site. Smaller footprint, shorter construction timelines, lower upfront capital, and in theory, the ability to learn and improve across a fleet of identical units.

The leading developers — Rolls-Royce in the UK, NuScale in the US, Terrestrial Energy in Canada, and several others — have been working toward this for over a decade. The first commercial SMR deployments are now coming into focus. Romania and the Czech Republic have moved forward with NuScale-based projects. The UK has shortlisted designs for government support. Poland, whose coal dependence has been an enormous obstacle to its climate targets, has moved with unexpected speed to embrace nuclear as the cornerstone of its energy transition.

I want to be clear-eyed about the challenges. SMR economics at scale remain unproven. The first units will be expensive, because they are first units. The regulatory frameworks in many countries are designed for conventional large plants and need adaptation. Public acceptance in some communities remains a genuine obstacle. And the waste question — while more manageable than often portrayed — still requires better-designed, longer-term solutions.

But the direction of travel is clear. The question is no longer whether SMRs will be commercially deployed. It is when, and at what cost curve.

## The Geopolitics of Nuclear

It would be naive to analyse the nuclear energy revival without acknowledging its geopolitical dimension. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 was a turning point. European countries that had relied on Russian gas — and some that had assumed Russian-linked nuclear fuel supply was stable — had a sharp lesson in the risks of energy dependency.

France, which had maintained the largest nuclear fleet in Europe throughout the decades when others were retreating, found itself in an unexpectedly strong position. Électricité de France has announced 14 new reactors. The political consensus in France — historically fractured on energy policy — has converged around nuclear with unusual unanimity.

For countries seeking energy independence, nuclear offers something that no other low-carbon technology does: the fuel cycle can be managed domestically. Uranium is available from multiple geopolitically stable sources — Canada, Australia, Kazakhstan, Namibia. Enrichment capacity is growing in allied nations. The strategic case for nuclear, layered on top of the climate and economic cases, creates a genuinely powerful combination.

## My Own Perspective

I'll be honest: I came to nuclear energy late. Like many people who care about climate change, my instinct for years was to focus on renewables and assume that nuclear's baggage — cost, waste, safety perception — made it the wrong horse to back. Working across the energy sector and engaging more deeply with the data changed my mind.

The numbers are simply too compelling. Nuclear has historically killed fewer people per unit of energy produced than any other major power source, including solar and wind (which carry their own occupational and manufacturing risks). The waste it produces, while genuinely hazardous and genuinely problematic, is measured in tonnes — not the billions of tonnes of CO2 that fossil fuels pump into the atmosphere.

The climate crisis requires us to deploy every credible low-carbon technology we have, as fast as we can. A world that meets its climate targets probably looks like more wind, more solar, more nuclear, more storage, and more grid interconnection working together — not any single technology winning.

What gives me genuine optimism about the nuclear revival is that it's being driven less by ideology and more by practical necessity. Governments, corporations, and investors are following the logic rather than the tradition. That's usually how the most durable industrial transitions actually happen.

Nuclear's comeback is real. It arrived more quietly than the solar story, without the dramatic cost curves and the Silicon Valley marketing. But it may end up being just as consequential.

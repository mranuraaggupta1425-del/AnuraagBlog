There's a phrase I keep hearing from founders right now: "it's never been easier to start, and it's never been harder to scale."

The first part is obviously true. The infrastructure for building a product in 2026 is almost absurdly accessible. You can prototype an application without writing a single line of code. You can run customer research with AI-assisted synthesis of interviews and surveys. You can launch a marketing campaign using generated content that, honestly, beats what most agencies produced five years ago. You can build customer support that handles thousands of queries without a support team. What used to require a seed round and a team of ten can now be done by one determined person with the right tools and a weekend of focus.

The second part — the difficulty of scaling — is less discussed, but it's the more important strategic reality of our moment. And I want to dig into both sides of this honestly, drawing on what I've observed working across European and Indian business environments over the past several years.

## The New Physics of Starting a Business

The venture capital model of the past decade was built on a particular assumption: that building required capital, capital was scarce, and therefore the founders who could raise the most money could outcompete those who couldn't. That assumption is collapsing under the weight of what AI has done to development and operational costs.

Consider what a solo founder or small team can now do at near-zero marginal cost: generate code, design user interfaces, write content, analyse data, conduct competitor research, draft legal documents, model financial projections, and deploy infrastructure. None of these are perfect AI outputs — judgment, iteration, and domain expertise still matter enormously. But the baseline capability threshold has dropped so dramatically that the minimum viable team to test a hypothesis has shrunk from ten people and $2 million to two people and $50,000.

This is not a marginal change. It's a structural shift in who can participate in entrepreneurship and how capital gets allocated across early-stage companies.

What it means practically: the period from "idea" to "something real people can use" has compressed from months to weeks. The ability to experiment more, fail faster, and iterate based on actual data rather than assumptions has become the central competitive advantage of the early stage.

## What Still Doesn't Change

And yet — the fundamentals haven't moved. The principles that separate businesses that last from those that flash and fade are the same as they were in 1990 or 1960.

**Customer obsession.** Every wave of technological change tempts founders to fall in love with the technology rather than the problem. AI is no different. The most dangerous trap for a 2026 founder is building an AI-powered solution looking for a problem rather than finding a genuine pain point and using AI as one tool to solve it. Customers don't care about your tech stack. They care whether their problem is solved.

**Unit economics.** I've seen too many businesses — and not just startups — ignore the fundamental question of whether the economics of serving each customer actually work. The ease of growth marketing, of viral loops, of free-tier acquisition strategies can mask a unit economics problem until it becomes a cash crisis. The discipline to measure and improve customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, and gross margin hasn't been automated away. It's more important than ever.

**Trust and reputation.** In a world where AI makes it trivially easy to generate content, communicate at scale, and replicate surface-level credibility, the genuine article — authentic expertise, demonstrated track record, real relationships — becomes more valuable, not less. This is counterintuitive but I'm increasingly convinced it's true.

## The Strategic Landscape Is Shifting

One of the most interesting competitive dynamics of 2026 is the relationship between startups and incumbents in an AI-saturated world. Historically, startups disrupted incumbents by moving faster, being leaner, and being willing to challenge assumptions the established players had calcified around. AI has changed this calculus in several ways.

Large incumbents can now deploy significant AI capabilities without the organisational drag that previously made them slow to adopt new technology. The gap between what a large enterprise can build and what a startup can build has narrowed in terms of raw product capability. What remains different — and this is where startups still have the edge — is speed of decision-making, willingness to kill failing experiments, and the ability to operate in genuinely new markets rather than protecting existing ones.

The strategic question for any founder is: where does my organisation's structure give me an advantage that a large incumbent genuinely cannot replicate? The answer is increasingly in **speed**, **focus**, and **genuine insight into an underserved market** — not in technological capability alone.

**Platform versus vertical.** The current strategic debate in startup land is whether to build horizontal AI tools that serve many industries, or vertical solutions that go deep into a specific sector. The conventional wisdom is shifting toward vertical. Horizontal AI tools are becoming commodities as foundation model providers build more of these capabilities into their base offerings. The businesses with durable margins are those that combine AI capabilities with **deep domain expertise** — in healthcare, in legal services, in construction, in agriculture — that competitors cannot easily replicate without years of domain investment.

## What I'd Tell a First-Time Founder Right Now

I spend time mentoring early-stage entrepreneurs, and the questions I get most often are about which tools to use, which markets to enter, and how to fundraise. My honest answers:

**On tools:** Use everything. Don't be precious about whether your code was AI-generated or your first draft was human-written. What matters is whether the output is good and what you learn from building it. The best founders I know are voracious experimenters with technology.

**On markets:** The best opportunities right now are in industries that are genuinely data-rich but have been slow to adopt modern software — manufacturing, agriculture, construction, professional services, logistics. These sectors have real pain, are willing to pay for real solutions, and haven't yet been picked clean by a decade of software venture investment.

**On fundraising:** The fundraising environment is more selective than 2021 but more rational. Investors want to see evidence — of customer demand, of unit economics, of a team that can execute. The "just build it and the money will come" era is over. The "show me why your specific team is uniquely positioned to win this specific market" era is very much here.

The underlying truth is that 2026 is a genuinely exciting moment to be building something. The tools available to founders today would have seemed like science fiction to the person who started a business ten years ago. But excitement about tools is not a strategy. Clarity about who you're serving, why you're the right team to serve them, and how the unit economics actually work — that's the unglamorous, irreplaceable foundation of every business that actually lasts.

I'm rooting for the builders. Always have been.

When ChatGPT launched at the end of 2022, it felt like a moment. Something had shifted. For the first time, a large language model was accessible, fluent, and genuinely useful to ordinary people — not just researchers. The world paid attention.

Three years on, the landscape looks completely different. The original ChatGPT moment has been followed by wave after wave of model releases, capability jumps, and competitive battles between the world's largest technology companies. And the really significant changes are not the ones that make the biggest headlines.

## The Capability Gap Has Closed — and Then Some

Early large language models were impressive but unreliable. They hallucinated confidently, struggled with reasoning tasks, and could not work with images, code, or real-time information in any meaningful way.

**That era is over.** The leading models in 2026 — across providers — can reason through multi-step problems, generate and debug complex code, analyse images and documents, and operate as autonomous agents that complete tasks across multiple tools and data sources.

The benchmark numbers tell part of the story. Models are now routinely passing professional exams in medicine, law, and engineering — not by memorising answers, but by demonstrating the ability to apply principles to novel problems. **Performance on reasoning benchmarks has improved by roughly 40%** over the past 18 months across the leading model families.

More importantly, **reliability has improved dramatically**. Hallucination rates — the tendency of models to invent information convincingly — have dropped sharply in the most capable systems, which has been the critical factor in enabling real-world enterprise deployment.

## The Shift From General to Specialised

One of the most significant trends of the past year has been the move toward **specialised models**. Rather than one large general-purpose model trying to do everything, the most effective deployments increasingly involve smaller, task-specific models that are faster, cheaper to run, and more accurate within their domain.

A hospital system, for example, might deploy a model fine-tuned specifically on medical literature, patient record formats, and clinical protocols — rather than a general model that knows a little about everything. A law firm might use a model trained on contract language, jurisdiction-specific case law, and regulatory frameworks.

This specialisation trend has opened the market to a much wider range of providers. **Mistral, Cohere, AI21 Labs**, and dozens of other companies have built thriving businesses around purpose-built models that outperform general systems in specific vertical markets, even if they cannot match the headline capability of the largest frontier models on broad benchmarks.

## Inference Cost and the Democratisation of AI

One number that does not get enough attention: **the cost of running AI has dropped by approximately 90%** over the past two years.

This matters enormously. When inference was expensive, AI deployments were limited to use cases where the value was large enough to justify the cost. Now, AI can be embedded into products and workflows where the per-query value is relatively small — customer support, document summarisation, search, content generation — because the cost is low enough to make the economics work.

For businesses, this means AI is no longer a strategic experiment. It is a baseline operational tool. Companies that have not integrated AI into their core workflows are not staying still — they are falling behind competitors who have.

For individuals, it means access to capable AI tools has become cheap and, in many cases, free. The global distribution of AI capability is becoming far more equitable than the early years suggested it would be.

## The Questions That Still Do Not Have Good Answers

For all the progress, several genuinely difficult questions remain unresolved.

**Alignment** — ensuring that AI systems reliably do what their operators intend, and not something subtly different — remains an active area of research without a definitive solution. As models become more capable and are given more autonomy, the stakes of misalignment increase.

**Energy consumption** is a growing concern. Training and running large models at scale requires significant computing power, and the electricity demands of AI infrastructure are putting pressure on power grids in several regions. The environmental cost of AI is a real consideration that the industry is grappling with, though progress on more efficient architectures is being made.

**Regulatory frameworks** are still catching up. Most jurisdictions have proposed AI regulations, but few have enacted comprehensive, enforceable rules. This creates uncertainty for developers and deployers alike, and the patchwork of different national approaches is creating compliance complexity for companies operating globally.

## Where This Is All Going

The direction of travel is clear, even if the exact timeline is not. Models will continue to get more capable, more efficient, and more deeply integrated into the infrastructure of daily life. The question of when — or whether — AI reaches human-level general intelligence remains genuinely open and genuinely contested among leading researchers.

What is not contested is that the next five years of AI development will have a larger practical impact on more industries and more people than the past five years. The foundation has been built. The deployment phase is well underway.

For anyone trying to navigate this landscape — whether as a business leader, a developer, a student, or simply a curious person — the most useful thing is to engage with AI tools directly. The gap between people who understand these systems through hands-on experience and those who only understand them through media coverage is wide, and it matters.

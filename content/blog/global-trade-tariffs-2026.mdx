In the late 1990s, there was a genuine consensus among economists, policymakers, and business leaders that free trade was the tide that lifted all boats. The logic was elegant: countries should produce what they're best at, trade freely with everyone else, and the global division of labour would make everyone richer. The World Trade Organisation was the institutional embodiment of that consensus. Globalisation was the word we used to describe its triumph.

That consensus is now in pieces on the floor. And understanding how we got here is essential to navigating what comes next — whether you're running a business, managing investments, or simply trying to make sense of inflation data that keeps surprising in the wrong direction.

## The Architecture of the New Protectionism

The current wave of trade barriers is different from previous cycles in an important way: it is being driven simultaneously from both ends of the political spectrum and across multiple geographies. This isn't a phase. It's a structural reorientation.

The United States, under successive administrations of both parties, has pursued an increasingly protectionist trade stance. The tariffs first imposed on Chinese goods in 2018 were never fully rolled back — they were extended and deepened. The CHIPS and Science Act, the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content requirements, and more recent executive actions have established a US industrial policy framework that explicitly prioritises domestic production over import competition in strategic sectors.

The logic is security-driven as much as economic. The COVID pandemic exposed the vulnerability of supply chains that had been optimised purely for cost efficiency. Semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, personal protective equipment, rare earth minerals — the list of things America discovered it couldn't reliably source from abroad in a crisis was sobering. The policy response was predictable.

The European Union has followed a similar path with different rhetoric. The Green Deal Industrial Plan and the Critical Raw Materials Act are designed to ensure that the EU is not dependent on any single country — particularly China — for the inputs of its energy transition. The EU has also introduced carbon border adjustment mechanisms, which are effectively tariffs on goods from countries without comparable carbon pricing. Whether you view this as climate policy or protectionism depends on your perspective, and the answer is probably "both."

China has responded with its own strategic self-sufficiency push. Xi Jinping's "dual circulation" strategy explicitly targets reducing China's dependence on foreign technology and markets while maintaining access to global export demand for Chinese goods. The investment in domestic semiconductor capacity, electric vehicles, batteries, and advanced manufacturing is enormous.

## The Winners and Losers Are Emerging

When major trade flows are disrupted, the map of winners and losers gets redrawn. Some of what we're seeing is genuinely interesting.

Mexico has been a significant beneficiary of US-China trade tensions through "nearshoring" — the relocation of manufacturing closer to end markets. FDI into Mexico's manufacturing sector has surged as companies seek to access the US market while reducing supply chain exposure to China. The auto industry, electronics, and medical devices have all driven significant investment. Mexico's geographic proximity to the US, its USMCA membership, and its competitive labour costs make it a natural alternative.

India has similarly benefited from supply chain diversification. Apple's aggressive push to manufacture iPhones in India — now producing a significant share of global iPhone output from Tamil Nadu and Karnataka — is the most visible example, but it's part of a broader pattern. Vietnam, Indonesia, and Malaysia have also attracted manufacturing investment that previously would have gone to China.

The European industrial heartland faces more structural pressure. German manufacturing — particularly the auto sector — is navigating a genuine existential challenge: the transition to electric vehicles favours Chinese battery technology, while the energy cost shock from the loss of cheap Russian gas has permanently impaired competitiveness in energy-intensive industries. The adjustments required are significant and politically difficult.

## What This Means for Prices — And for All of Us

Here's the uncomfortable truth about protectionism that economists have been consistent about even when politicians haven't: it generally raises prices for consumers.

Tariffs are, in effect, a tax on imports. That tax is typically shared between foreign exporters (who accept lower margins) and domestic consumers (who pay more). The protection it affords domestic producers comes at the cost of economic efficiency. The sectors shielded from competition may preserve employment in the short term, but often at the cost of innovation and productivity growth over the longer term.

The inflationary pressure from the current wave of protectionism is real and measurable. Domestic semiconductor production in the US, while strategically important, costs significantly more per unit than Taiwanese production. The domestic content requirements in electric vehicle subsidies have made some EVs more expensive than their imported alternatives. These costs accumulate quietly but persistently.

I'm not arguing that the strategic case for industrial policy is wrong. Comparative advantage in the classical sense doesn't capture national security considerations, climate transition requirements, or the importance of maintaining critical manufacturing capabilities. The case for strategic autonomy in semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, and clean energy has genuine merit. The question is whether we're being honest about the trade-offs.

## The WTO and the Rules-Based Trading System

One of the most significant — and least discussed — features of the current environment is the progressive weakening of the WTO as an effective dispute resolution forum. The WTO's Appellate Body has been effectively paralysed since 2019, when the United States blocked the appointment of new judges in protest of what it viewed as judicial overreach. Without a functioning appeals mechanism, trade disputes limp along in procedural limbo rather than getting resolved.

This matters because it means the rules-based trading system increasingly lacks meaningful enforcement. Countries can impose tariffs that violate WTO commitments and face no effective remedy for years. The incentive to comply with the rules erodes when non-compliance carries no real cost.

## How Businesses Are Actually Adapting

Having spent time working across EMEA markets, I've watched companies navigate trade uncertainty with varying degrees of sophistication. The ones adapting most effectively share some common characteristics.

They're treating supply chain geography as a strategic asset rather than a pure cost optimisation problem. The "China plus one" or "China plus two" strategy — maintaining China operations while building parallel capacity in Southeast Asia or South Asia — has become standard among multinationals of scale. It costs more. It's worth it.

They're monitoring trade policy with the same seriousness as market risk. The companies caught flat-footed by tariff announcements are increasingly the exception. The sophistication of trade policy monitoring — using specialist advisers, government affairs teams, and industry associations — has improved substantially.

They're building flexibility into procurement contracts. Fixed, long-term supplier relationships optimised for a stable trade environment have given way to more dynamic arrangements that preserve optionality.

The era of globalisation that defined the last three decades isn't reversing entirely — trade still flows in vast quantities, and the mutual dependencies between economies are too deep to unwind quickly. But the rules, the assumptions, and the institutions governing that trade are all in flux simultaneously. For businesses, for policymakers, and for investors, the capacity to read that flux accurately has rarely been more valuable.

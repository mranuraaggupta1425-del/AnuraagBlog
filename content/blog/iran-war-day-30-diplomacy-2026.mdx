Thirty days into the US-Israel strikes on Iran, the conflict shows no signs of a clean resolution — but the first serious diplomatic push is now underway, and the world is watching carefully to see whether it holds any real weight.

Foreign ministers from **Pakistan, Turkey, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia** convened in Islamabad over the weekend for emergency talks aimed at brokering a ceasefire. It is the most significant diplomatic gathering since the war began on February 28, and it signals that regional powers — even those aligned with Washington in other contexts — are increasingly alarmed by how far this conflict has spread.

## The Diplomatic Push: Who Is Talking and Why

The Islamabad summit is not an official UN-backed peace process. It is a regional effort, driven by countries that share borders, trade routes, and populations deeply affected by the war's ripple effects.

**Turkey** has positioned itself as a natural mediator. It is a NATO member with close ties to the West, but has also maintained working relationships with Tehran and has historically played both sides of Middle Eastern conflicts with considerable skill. President Erdogan's government has been vocal about opposing the strikes since day one.

**Saudi Arabia** finds itself in an awkward position. US forces are stationed on its soil, and an Iranian strike on a Saudi airbase wounded **15 American service members** last week — a direct escalation that pulled Riyadh further into a war it did not start. Saudi Arabia's participation in these talks reflects a desire to find an exit before the conflict escalates further into its own territory.

**Pakistan and Egypt** bring numbers and regional credibility to the table. Both have large Muslim-majority populations with strong public sentiment against the war, and both governments face domestic pressure to take a visible stance.

The question is whether any of these countries can actually deliver a ceasefire — and the honest answer is that their leverage over Washington is limited.

## What Is Happening on the Ground

The military situation has not stabilised. Over the past 72 hours:

- **Houthi forces in Yemen** fired multiple missiles toward Israel, with most intercepted by Israeli air defences.
- **Iranian-backed militias in Iraq** have been clashing with US forces, with several incidents reported near Baghdad's airport corridor.
- **A senior Iranian official** publicly accused the US of planning a ground invasion as a "next stage" of the conflict — a claim the Pentagon has not directly addressed.
- **Iranian oil infrastructure** has been partially targeted, though the Strait of Hormuz remains open under President Trump's extended deadline.

The threat of a ground invasion — real or as a negotiating tactic — has significantly raised the stakes. Iran's military doctrine has long been built around asymmetric warfare, proxy networks, and the ability to inflict cost without conventional confrontation. A ground operation would be a different kind of war entirely.

## Oil, Shipping and the Economic Pressure Building

The global economy is absorbing real damage. **Brent crude has held above $105 per barrel** for the past two weeks, a level not seen since 2022. Shipping insurance costs for vessels transiting the Persian Gulf have surged by over **300%** since the conflict began, and several major shipping companies have rerouted vessels around the Cape of Good Hope rather than through the Gulf — adding weeks to journey times and significant costs.

European industrial output has been affected. German manufacturing has seen energy cost increases that are now showing up in factory output data. India, which imports a significant portion of its oil from the Gulf, has been drawing down strategic reserves to buffer against price shocks.

The economic pain is not yet catastrophic, but it is cumulative. **Every week the conflict continues**, the damage to global supply chains, energy prices, and investor confidence compounds.

## Pope Leo XIV and the Moral Voice

On Palm Sunday, **Pope Leo XIV** addressed tens of thousands in St. Peter's Square, rejecting claims that God could ever justify war and praying specifically for Christians in the Middle East — a community caught in a conflict they have no part in and no power to stop.

It was a reminder that beyond the geopolitical calculations and energy market data, there are millions of ordinary people — Iranian, Saudi, Iraqi, Yemeni, Israeli — whose daily lives have been upended by decisions made in capitals far from their homes.

## What Happens Next

Three scenarios are being discussed in diplomatic and analyst circles.

**A negotiated pause**: The Islamabad talks could produce a framework for a temporary ceasefire, contingent on Iran agreeing to inspection of nuclear facilities and the US agreeing to halt strikes. This is the most optimistic scenario and, currently, the least likely — neither side has shown the political will to accept the other's conditions.

**Prolonged stalemate**: The conflict settles into a lower-intensity but persistent phase — airstrikes, proxy skirmishes, economic pressure — without a clear resolution. This is the scenario most analysts currently consider most probable.

**Escalation**: A ground operation, a Strait of Hormuz closure, or a major miscalculation pushes the conflict into territory that forces a broader international response. This remains a tail risk, but one that markets and governments are pricing in.

The next two weeks will be telling. The Islamabad talks are a beginning, not an answer — but after 30 days of conflict, even a beginning is worth something.

My grandfather died of a heart attack at 62. His father died of a heart attack at 58. For much of my life, that family history felt like an inheritance I couldn't refuse — a biological sentence written before I was born. What's happening in biotech and medicine right now makes me genuinely reconsider that assumption. And that, more than any specific drug or technology, is what excites me most about this moment.

We are living through a compression of medical progress that would have seemed implausible to the scientists who shaped the last century. Breakthroughs that would once have taken decades from laboratory discovery to clinical application are moving in years. The tools available to researchers — AI-assisted drug design, CRISPR gene editing, protein structure prediction, mRNA platforms — have changed the fundamental economics and timelines of medicine. What this means for human health, human lifespan, and the global burden of disease is difficult to overstate.

## The GLP-1 Story: More Than a Weight Loss Drug

If you've followed healthcare news over the past two years, you've encountered the GLP-1 revolution. Drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) began as diabetes treatments and have become the most commercially successful pharmaceutical products in history by treating something medicine long struggled to address: obesity.

The clinical results are remarkable. These drugs, which mimic gut hormones that regulate appetite and blood sugar, produce sustained weight loss of 15–25% in clinical trials — outcomes that were previously only achievable through bariatric surgery. But the story goes far beyond weight. Cardiovascular outcomes trials showed that semaglutide reduced major adverse cardiac events by 20% in people with obesity and established cardiovascular disease. Trials exploring effects on kidney disease, liver disease, sleep apnoea, Alzheimer's, and addiction are producing genuinely surprising positive data.

What we appear to be discovering is that GLP-1 receptors — the molecular targets these drugs act on — are distributed throughout the body in ways that interact with far more biological systems than we initially appreciated. The cardiometabolic benefits appear to exceed what you'd predict from weight loss alone. Researchers are now asking a question that would have sounded absurd five years ago: is this drug class a broad-spectrum treatment for the chronic diseases of ageing?

I raise this not to oversell pharmaceutical products — the side effects are real, the access disparities are stark, and the long-term data is still accumulating. But the GLP-1 story illustrates something important about the current moment in medicine: we are repeatedly discovering that our models of how the body works are incomplete, and new tools are revealing connections we didn't know existed.

## CRISPR and the Gene Editing Era

The first CRISPR-based medicine — a treatment for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia called Casgevy — received approval in late 2023. It has since been joined by additional gene therapy approvals across multiple conditions. We are now, undeniably, in the era of gene editing medicine.

CRISPR's promise is profound: the ability to make precise edits to the genetic code of living cells, correcting the mutations that cause hereditary disease. Sickle cell, haemophilia, certain forms of blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy — conditions that have been managed but never cured are now in the crosshairs of therapies that act on root causes rather than symptoms.

![DNA double helix — the blueprint of life being rewritten by modern biotechnology](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1559757148-5c350d0d3c56?w=1200&q=80)

The technology is evolving rapidly. Base editing — a refinement of CRISPR that makes single-letter changes to the genetic code without cutting the DNA strand — is showing impressive results in lowering LDL cholesterol through a single injection in early trials. Prime editing, which can make more complex genetic corrections, is advancing toward clinical programmes. In vivo gene editing — delivering CRISPR directly into the body rather than editing cells outside and reinfusing them — is moving from animal models toward human trials.

The costs, which were prohibitive for initial approvals, are expected to decline significantly as manufacturing scales and competition emerges. The regulatory frameworks are still developing, and appropriately so. But the trajectory is clear.

I find myself genuinely grappling with where this technology goes over the next decade. Treating disease is unambiguously positive. The more complex questions involve enhancement — using genetic tools not to correct disease but to modify normal human traits. Those conversations are happening in bioethics circles now, and they will move into mainstream policy debates faster than most people expect.

## AI Meets Drug Discovery

The integration of AI into pharmaceutical research deserves its own headline because it is genuinely changing the timelines and economics of drug development.

DeepMind's AlphaFold — the AI system that can predict the three-dimensional structure of proteins from their amino acid sequences with remarkable accuracy — was one of the most significant scientific breakthroughs of the 2020s. Protein structure prediction, which previously required years of painstaking laboratory work, can now be done computationally in hours. The implications for drug discovery are enormous: understanding how a drug molecule interacts with its protein target requires knowing what that target looks like, and AlphaFold has opened that door for essentially every protein in the human body.

AI systems are now being used to design novel drug molecules with desired properties, predict how they will interact with off-target proteins (which causes side effects), model their metabolism and excretion, and identify patient populations most likely to respond. The pharmaceutical companies and biotech startups investing heavily in AI-driven drug discovery are reporting compressed timelines from initial discovery to lead compound identification.

This doesn't eliminate the challenge of clinical trials — the human body still surprises us, and validating safety and efficacy in humans is irreducible. But it changes the denominator: if you can screen ten times as many candidate molecules computationally before committing to expensive laboratory and clinical work, the odds of finding something that works improve materially.

## The Access Problem We Can't Afford to Ignore

Here is the question that sits uncomfortably at the centre of this optimistic picture: for whom is the biotech revolution happening?

The CRISPR sickle cell treatment costs approximately $2.2 million per patient. GLP-1 drugs cost $1,000 or more per month without insurance, and payer coverage remains inconsistent. Advanced cancer immunotherapies regularly exceed $100,000 per treatment course. The leading-edge interventions of modern medicine are concentrated in wealthy countries and, within those countries, increasingly inaccessible to those without excellent insurance or significant personal wealth.

![Modern hospital facility — where cutting-edge medicine meets the challenge of equitable access](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1519494026892-80bbd2d6fd0d?w=1200&q=80)

This is not a new problem in medicine, and it is not unique to biotech. But it becomes more acute as the treatments become more transformative. A world in which effective cures for genetic disease, obesity, and cardiovascular disease exist but are available only to the wealthy is not a world that has solved those problems. It is a world that has added them to the growing list of inequalities that compound across generations.

The innovation is genuinely extraordinary. The business models that deliver it to patients need to catch up. That will require better global procurement mechanisms, more aggressive IP licensing for developing countries, public investment in generic manufacturing, and honest conversations about whether the current pharmaceutical pricing system is fit for an era of curative medicines.

I don't have simple answers to those questions. But I refuse to celebrate the science without acknowledging the distribution problem sitting beneath it.

We are living through a genuinely remarkable chapter in medicine. The tools available to fight disease have leapt forward in ways that will, over the coming decades, dramatically reduce human suffering. The challenge now is ensuring that "reduce human suffering" is understood broadly rather than narrowly — and that the extraordinary progress of the laboratory makes its way, fairly, to the full breadth of human need.

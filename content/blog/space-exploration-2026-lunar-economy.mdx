I've always thought the best way to understand the future is to watch where the serious money is going. Not the speculative kind — the kind backed by sovereign wealth funds, government programmes, and executives who answer to shareholders. By that measure, the message from 2026 is clear: the Moon is open for business.

This isn't the Apollo era, where a single superpower poured resources into a geopolitical race to plant a flag. What's happening now is structurally different — and in many ways, far more interesting.

![The Moon rising over the horizon — a new frontier for commerce and science](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1451187580459-43490279c0fa?w=1200&q=80)

## The Infrastructure Race Is Already Underway

NASA's Artemis programme, despite its share of delays and budget battles, has fundamentally changed the conversation. The Lunar Gateway — the orbiting space station designed to serve as a staging point for lunar surface missions — has now secured international partners, with ESA, JAXA, and the Canadian Space Agency all contributing modules and capabilities. This is not just a science programme. It is the first piece of genuine space infrastructure designed with commercial access in mind.

SpaceX's Starship has become the workhorse of the new lunar economy. Its ability to carry massive payloads to the Moon — and eventually to refuel in lunar orbit using propellant manufactured on the surface — changes the cost calculus of everything that follows. When the marginal cost of getting hardware to the Moon drops by an order of magnitude, activities that were previously theoretical become financially viable.

The competition isn't only American. China's CNSA has been executing its lunar programme with a discipline and pace that deserves more credit than it typically gets in Western coverage. Chang'e-6 returned samples from the Moon's far side. Chang'e-7 is being prepared to survey the lunar south pole — the area most likely to contain water ice deposits. And China has made clear its intention to have a permanent crewed presence on the Moon by the early 2030s. This is not science fiction. This is a scheduled programme.

## Water Ice Changes Everything

The reason everyone is focused on the lunar south pole is straightforward: water ice. Multiple missions have confirmed its presence in permanently shadowed craters near the poles. That water can be split into hydrogen and oxygen — the core ingredients of rocket propellant. In other words, the Moon may contain the resources needed to refuel spacecraft *in space*, dramatically reducing the cost and energy required to travel deeper into the solar system.

![Permanently shadowed craters at the lunar south pole — where water ice may hold the key to deep space exploration](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1446776811953-b23d57bd21aa?w=1200&q=80)

This has profound implications. Mars missions that currently require everything to be launched from Earth's gravity well could instead refuel at the Moon. Commercial space stations positioned in lunar orbit could be serviced from lunar surface resources rather than relying solely on Earth launches. The Moon, in this framework, stops being a destination and becomes a gateway.

There are also rare earth minerals and helium-3 to consider. The latter is a potential fusion reactor fuel that exists in abundance on the lunar surface, having been deposited there by the solar wind over billions of years. Whether fusion technology ever matures to the point where helium-3 mining becomes commercially relevant is speculative — but the option has value, and option value is something markets understand very well.

## Who Owns What on the Moon?

Here's where things get complicated. The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 — the foundational legal framework for activities in space — was written in a very different era. It prohibits national appropriation of the Moon, but says nothing clear about the commercial extraction of lunar resources by private companies operating under national licences.

The United States addressed this ambiguity through the Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015, which explicitly allows American citizens and companies to own resources they extract from space, even if not the territory itself. Several other countries, including Luxembourg, the UAE, and Japan, have passed similar domestic frameworks. China has not.

The Artemis Accords — currently signed by over 40 nations — attempt to establish norms around resource extraction, transparency, and the designation of "safety zones" around lunar operations. But they are not a treaty. They have no enforcement mechanism. And crucially, China and Russia have not signed.

The legal vacuum around lunar governance is one of the most underappreciated risks in the emerging space economy. I've spent enough time in international business to know that commercial activity without clear legal frameworks tends to produce disputes, mistrust, and missed opportunities. Getting the rules right before the scramble begins is far easier than trying to establish them after.

## The Business Models Are Getting Real

What strikes me most about the current moment is how concrete the commercial propositions are becoming. Intuitive Machines has already landed commercial payloads on the lunar surface. Astrobotic is developing autonomous mining prototypes. ispace — a Japanese startup that came agonisingly close to a successful lunar landing in 2023 before losing contact at the final moment — pressed forward and launched again. The willingness to absorb failure, iterate, and return is the signature of a genuine industry rather than a vanity project.

Beyond direct lunar activities, the broader space economy is growing rapidly. Earth observation satellites provide intelligence to agriculture, insurance, and defence. In-space manufacturing — producing pharmaceuticals and fibre optic cables in microgravity — is moving from research to early commercialisation. Space tourism, while still the preserve of the extremely wealthy, is establishing the business model and the safety record that will eventually make it more accessible.

## Why This Matters Beyond the Astronauts

I want to push back gently against the view that space exploration is a luxury — something we should only pursue once we've solved our problems on Earth. That framing, while emotionally intuitive, misunderstands how civilisations and technologies actually develop.

The GPS system you use to navigate your commute was a space programme. The satellite communication infrastructure underpinning global finance is a space programme. The climate monitoring that tracks glaciers, hurricanes, and crop failures is a space programme. The technologies developed for space — in materials science, energy systems, life support, remote sensing, and miniaturisation — have consistently found their way into everyday life in ways nobody predicted at the outset.

The lunar economy will be no different. The pressure to solve problems in the extreme environment of space — energy storage, water recycling, radiation shielding, remote autonomous operations — will generate breakthroughs with applications far beyond the Moon.

There's also something I'll admit is harder to quantify but feels important: the ambition itself matters. Civilisations that look outward, that invest in understanding and expanding their frontiers, tend to develop differently than those that don't. I want to live in a world that is still curious enough about what lies beyond to go and find out.

The lunar economy is no longer science fiction. It's a strategic competition, a commercial opportunity, and a test of whether humanity can extend its cooperative instincts beyond the atmosphere. I'm watching it closely — and I suspect anyone interested in the shape of the next century should be too.

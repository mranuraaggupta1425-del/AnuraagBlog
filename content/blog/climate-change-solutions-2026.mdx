There's a particular kind of exhaustion that comes from following climate news. The reports get more alarming, the records keep breaking, the summits conclude with pledges that feel simultaneously necessary and inadequate. I understand why people oscillate between anxiety and numbness. I've felt both.

But I want to offer a different lens in this post — not the comforting kind that papers over real danger, and not the doom-scrolling kind that paralyses rather than motivates. The climate story of 2026 is genuinely complicated: the bad news is worse than most people realise, and the good news is more substantial than it gets credit for. Both things are true simultaneously, and holding that tension is, I think, the only intellectually honest place to stand.

![Vast solar farm stretching across a sun-drenched landscape](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1509391366360-2e959784a276?w=1200&q=80)

## The Bad News, Plainly Stated

Let's not flinch. The scientific community's assessment is unambiguous: we are in the early stages of dangerous climate disruption, and the window for preventing the worst outcomes is narrowing.

Global average temperature has now exceeded 1.5°C of warming above pre-industrial levels in multiple consecutive years — the threshold the Paris Agreement was designed to avoid. This doesn't mean the 1.5°C goal is irrelevant (stabilising warming at any level is better than allowing it to continue rising), but it does mean that some consequences we hoped to prevent are now locked in.

The practical manifestations are not abstract. **Extreme weather events** — heatwaves, floods, droughts, and intense tropical cyclones — have increased in frequency and severity in ways that directly match climate model predictions. The 2025 European summer heat event killed thousands and caused €180 billion in economic damage. Monsoon flooding across South and Southeast Asia displaced millions. The Australian bushfire season has become almost permanent. These are not isolated incidents. They are the new normal.

**Sea level rise** is accelerating. Antarctic and Greenland ice sheet dynamics are tracking toward the more pessimistic end of scientific projections. Coastal cities from Mumbai to Miami to Jakarta are beginning to confront adaptation costs measured in the trillions. Some low-lying island nations are now engaged in active planning for managed retreat — the polite term for the abandonment of inhabited land.

**Ecosystem collapse** is less visible but equally consequential. Coral bleaching has now affected the majority of the world's reef systems. Permafrost thaw in the Arctic is releasing methane — a potent greenhouse gas — at rates that create feedback loops the models are still struggling to fully capture.

## The Good News, Which Is Real

![Wind turbines generating clean power against a blue sky](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1470115636492-6d2b56f9146d?w=1200&q=80)

And yet. Something meaningful is also happening on the other side of the ledger, and it deserves acknowledgement.

**Renewable energy has won the economic argument.** Solar and wind are now the cheapest forms of new electricity generation in most of the world — not because of subsidies alone, but because the technology has matured, supply chains have scaled, and learning curves have driven costs down faster than almost anyone predicted. In 2025, more than 60% of new global power generation capacity came from renewables. That's extraordinary.

**Electric vehicle adoption is accelerating past the projections.** In Europe, EVs now represent over 40% of new car sales. In China, the figure is higher. India, long seen as a laggard, is seeing rapid uptake driven by a combination of government incentives, falling battery costs, and domestic manufacturing ambition. The internal combustion engine has a future, but it's shortening every quarter.

**Carbon markets, despite their imperfections, are maturing.** The EU Emissions Trading System has shown that you can price carbon in ways that drive real industrial change. New compliance markets in North America, Southeast Asia, and the Gulf are adding to the global coverage. Voluntary carbon markets remain messy and susceptible to fraud, but the regulatory frameworks catching up to them.

**Technology breakthroughs are happening faster than expected.** Green hydrogen production costs are falling. Direct air carbon capture, while still expensive, is reaching deployment scale rather than remaining in the lab. Long-duration energy storage — the piece that makes variable renewables dependable — is seeing genuine commercial traction. Battery technology continues to improve on multiple fronts simultaneously.

## The Policy Landscape in 2026

The geopolitics of climate have shifted in interesting ways. The old binary — US versus China, developed world versus developing world — is being replaced by something more complex.

The US Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, despite being debated and modified since, has unleashed over a trillion dollars in clean energy investment in North America. The EU's Green Deal continues to drive industrial transformation, even as political headwinds create pressure to soften some timelines. India's solar programme has become one of the world's most ambitious deployment stories.

China remains the most complicated variable. It is simultaneously the world's largest emitter, its largest manufacturer of clean energy equipment, and the country deploying more renewable capacity than any other. Its engagement with global climate frameworks is transactional and strategic rather than ideological — which makes prediction difficult but also creates room for pragmatic collaboration where interests align.

The developing world dynamic is where the real moral weight lies. Countries least responsible for historical emissions are bearing disproportionate climate impacts. The pledges made at successive COP summits on climate finance for developing nations have been repeatedly unfulfilled. This is not a minor footnote — it's a central tension in the global climate conversation, and one that will determine whether effective international cooperation is achievable.

## What I Think the Honest Summary Is

We are not on track to prevent serious climate disruption. That's the honest read of the data. But we are on track to prevent the absolute worst scenarios, if the trajectory of clean energy deployment continues and political commitment doesn't collapse.

The difference between 2°C and 3°C of warming is not a rounding error. It's tens of millions of additional people displaced. It's ecosystems that survive versus those that collapse. It's the difference between managed difficulty and cascading catastrophe. Every fraction of a degree that additional action prevents is worth fighting for.

What I find myself coming back to is the agency question. Climate can feel like a problem so large that individual action is meaningless and collective action is impossible. I don't think either is true.

Individual action — particularly around consumption, diet, mobility, and how we vote and invest — does aggregate into signals that drive market and political change. It also matters for the integrity of the argument: it's harder to dismiss climate concern as performative when the people expressing it are actually changing their behaviour.

Collective action — through policy, through corporate commitments that go beyond greenwashing, through the decisions of cities and states and pension funds — is where the scale of change actually lives. And I'm seeing more of it than the headlines suggest.

The climate story of 2026 is not over. In many ways, the most important chapters are still being written — in engineering labs, in legislative chambers, in the decisions of investors, and in the preferences of billions of consumers. I'd rather be part of writing those chapters than just reading about them.

Prague played host to one of figure skating's most compelling World Championships in years. The 2026 edition delivered edge-of-the-seat performances, unexpected results, and a statement from the next generation of skaters that the sport's future is in very capable hands.

For American fans, the story of these championships begins with two names: **Amber Glenn** and **Ilia Malinin**. Both arrived in Prague carrying the weight of disappointing Olympic finishes last month. Both used the World Championships as a chance to remind the world exactly what they are capable of.

## Ilia Malinin: The Skater Rewriting the Rulebook

Ilia Malinin is doing things on ice that were considered theoretical a decade ago. The 21-year-old American has built his entire competitive identity around the **quadruple axel** — a jump with **four and a half rotations** that no skater in history had successfully landed in competition before Malinin came along.

At these World Championships, Malinin delivered a free skate that left the Prague crowd on its feet. His technical base score is the highest in the current generation of men's singles skaters, and when he is on — when the jumps are clean and the skating between them flows — he is genuinely unlike anything the sport has seen.

The Olympics result stung. He was not at his best on the biggest stage, and he knew it. Prague felt like a response. Skaters of Malinin's profile rarely stay quiet for long after a disappointing performance, and that competitive edge showed in every element of his programme.

## Amber Glenn: Resilience as a Competitive Strategy

Amber Glenn's story is different in character but equally compelling. She is a skater who has faced genuine adversity — not just competitive setbacks, but personal ones — and has consistently found a way to come back stronger.

Her programmes at these World Championships showed a skater in full command of her artistic identity. Glenn is not trying to win on technical difficulty alone; she builds performances that connect with audiences emotionally, and the judges reward that connection.

After the Olympics, where the pressure of the moment seemed to affect her execution, Prague represented a chance to skate freely. The difference was visible. **Freed from the weight of an Olympic cycle**, she produced the kind of skating that originally put her on the world stage.

The women's competition at these championships was exceptionally deep. Skaters from South Korea, Japan, and Russia (competing under neutral status) were all producing performances of the highest quality, making every fraction of a point matter.

## The Bigger Picture: Figure Skating's Global Reach

Figure skating has always been a sport with a passionate but niche audience in many countries. What has changed in recent years is the globalisation of its competitive base.

**Japan alone** has produced a generation of skaters — male and female — who have transformed the technical and artistic standards of the sport. The country's skating infrastructure, coaching depth, and cultural support for the sport have made it a dominant force. South Korean skating has followed a similar trajectory.

The US has historically been strong in individual events, but the current generation — led by Malinin — represents something of a technical revolution. The conversation around what jumps are possible, what programmes can contain, has shifted significantly in the past five years.

What all of this means for audiences is more competition, more unpredictability, and more of the genuine drama that makes live sport compelling. The Prague championships delivered exactly that.

## The Road Ahead

The 2026 World Championships serve as a reset point in the quad cycle. The next Winter Olympics are four years away, and the skaters who will dominate that competition may not yet be the ones at the top of the standings today.

What Prague confirmed is that the technical ceiling of figure skating is still rising. Malinin's quadruple axel was once a landmark. Now it is an expectation. The question being asked in coaches' circles across the world is: **what comes next?**

For Amber Glenn, the goal is consistency — building on Prague's momentum through the off-season and arriving at next season's Grand Prix series as a genuine title contender. For Malinin, the challenge is combining his extraordinary technical arsenal with the performance maturity that Olympic champions tend to possess.

Both stories are worth following closely. Figure skating rarely provides clean narratives, but it consistently provides memorable ones.

I grew up in a system where a single teacher managed forty students, where access to good instruction depended heavily on your postcode, and where the difference between a student who thrived and one who fell behind often came down to luck — the luck of having a parent who could help with homework, or a tutor, or a school that happened to have exceptional staff.

That system still exists in large parts of the world. But it's being disrupted in ways that were genuinely unimaginable even five years ago. The EdTech revolution of 2026 isn't primarily about putting tablets in classrooms or digitising textbooks. It's about **personalisation at scale** — the ability to give each learner the kind of attentive, adaptive, responsive instruction that was previously available only to the privileged few who could afford private tutoring.

That's a big claim. Let me try to make it real.

## What's Actually Changed

The breakthrough that underlies most of the meaningful progress in EdTech over the past two years is the same one driving transformation across industries: large language models that can engage in genuine, contextual, adaptive dialogue.

Prior generations of EdTech were largely digitised versions of existing instruction — video lectures, multiple choice assessments, progress tracking dashboards. These were improvements over nothing, but they shared the fundamental limitation of traditional instruction: one size, many students.

What AI tutoring systems can now do is qualitatively different. When a student gets a concept wrong, an AI tutor doesn't just mark it incorrect and move on. It probes to understand *where* the misunderstanding lies — is it a gap in prerequisite knowledge? A misconception formed from an earlier lesson? A problem of application rather than understanding? And it adjusts its subsequent instruction accordingly, in real time, for each individual student.

This sounds clinical but the experience — particularly for learners who have struggled in traditional environments — can feel transformative. Students who have never had anyone notice that they were confused about the foundation of a topic, who have learned to mask their gaps rather than address them, suddenly encounter instruction that meets them where they actually are.

**Duolingo's transformation** from a language app into a full AI-powered tutoring platform is an example people can actually see and use. Khan Academy's Khanmigo, built on top of large language models, has demonstrated measurable learning gains in controlled studies. Dozens of smaller platforms are competing in specific subject areas — mathematics, coding, test preparation, professional skill development — with increasingly sophisticated adaptive systems.

## The Equity Dimension

![Students in a rural school using connected tablets for digital learning](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1513542789411-b6a5d4f31634?w=1200&q=80)

The equity potential of AI-powered education is the part of this story I find most compelling — and most important to examine carefully.

The optimistic case: a student in a rural district in Bihar, or a township outside Johannesburg, or a remote community in Indonesia can now access a quality of personalised instruction that was previously available only to families wealthy enough to hire private tutors. The AI doesn't care about your geography. It doesn't have capacity constraints. It doesn't have off days. The levelling potential is real.

The cautious case: access to the devices and connectivity required to use these tools remains profoundly unequal. The students most likely to benefit from AI tutoring are, in the near term, those who already have smartphones, reliable internet, and parents who know how to navigate these platforms. The students who could benefit most — those in resource-poor environments with the largest gaps in educational access — face barriers that are infrastructural and economic before they're technological.

This doesn't negate the opportunity. It defines the challenge. Governments, NGOs, and EdTech companies that are serious about equity need to invest as heavily in connectivity and device access as they do in developing the AI tools themselves. The technology is ahead of the infrastructure in too many of the places where it could do the most good.

**India's situation** is instructive and close to my own experience. The country has made genuine strides in educational technology deployment — DIKSHA, iGOT, and various state-level initiatives have put digital content into the hands of millions of students. But the variance in outcomes between connected urban learners and those in connectivity-challenged rural districts remains stark. Closing that gap requires investment that goes beyond the EdTech sector's incentive structure.

## What's Happening in Higher Education

Universities are navigating an existential moment. The traditional value proposition — four years in a specific location, a credential at the end, and the social capital of a particular alumni network — is being challenged from multiple directions simultaneously.

**Online credentialing and micro-credentials** from platforms like Coursera, edX, LinkedIn Learning, and Google Career Certificates are gaining labour market acceptance in ways that would have seemed unlikely five years ago. Employers who previously used degree credentials as a filtering mechanism are increasingly open to portfolios, demonstrated skills, and alternative credentials — particularly in technology fields where practical capability is demonstrable.

**AI-assisted learning in university contexts** is generating institutional anxiety and genuine opportunity in equal measure. The plagiarism panic of 2023 and 2024 — as AI writing tools made it trivially easy to submit generated essays — has forced a genuine rethink of what assessment actually measures and why. The institutions that have responded well are redesigning assessment toward authentic tasks, oral examination, collaborative projects, and demonstrated application — things that are harder to outsource to an AI and more valuable to employers anyway.

**Internationalisation** is being reshaped by both digital tools and post-pandemic changes in student mobility. While the demand for international higher education experience hasn't disappeared, the willingness to pay premium prices for credentials that can increasingly be obtained digitally at a fraction of the cost is under sustained pressure. Universities whose reputation rests primarily on credential prestige rather than research quality or genuine learning outcomes are under the most strategic stress.

## The Questions That Matter Most

![Teacher mentoring a student, demonstrating the irreplaceable human element of education](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1544717305-2782549b5136?w=1200&q=80)

The deeper conversation that the EdTech boom is forcing — and that doesn't happen enough in the breathless coverage of the technology itself — is about what education is fundamentally for.

If education is primarily about transmitting information and certifying that someone has received it, then AI systems are genuinely superior at this task than most human instruction. They're more patient, more consistent, more adaptive, and available 24/7.

But most educators and parents would say education is about more than this. It's about developing the capacity to think critically, to collaborate, to navigate uncertainty, to build relationships, to form and defend reasoned positions, to experience the discipline of sustained effort. These are things that happen in human contexts — in classrooms where a teacher notices a student's distress, in group projects where conflict has to be navigated, in extracurricular activities where failure has real stakes.

The strongest vision for AI in education isn't AI replacing teachers. It's AI handling the instructional tasks that can be individualised and automated, freeing teachers to focus on the human dimensions of their work — mentorship, motivation, social development, the things that require genuine human presence and connection.

Whether that vision is realised depends on decisions being made right now by governments, school systems, and EdTech companies. Are AI tools being deployed in ways that enhance teacher capacity, or in ways that reduce headcount? Are the productivity gains from AI in education being reinvested into better support for students and teachers, or extracted as profit?

These are not technology questions. They are political and ethical ones. And the answers will determine whether the EdTech revolution of 2026 is genuinely transformative for learners — or just transformative for the investors backing the platforms.

I'm watching closely. And I think the rest of us should be too.

Markets in 2026 have been anything but quiet. After a strong close to 2025, the first quarter of this year has brought a sharp reminder that equities do not move in straight lines — and that macro events can override even the most carefully constructed investment thesis.

The US-Iran conflict, persistent inflation in parts of Europe, a resurgent dollar, and a wave of AI-driven corporate restructuring have all contributed to a market environment that rewards patience and punishes panic in equal measure. Here is a clear look at what is happening, what is driving it, and what seasoned investors are watching carefully.

## The Big Picture: Where Markets Stand

The S&P 500 has given back roughly **8% from its January 2026 peak**, largely driven by energy price shocks following the outbreak of conflict in the Middle East. The **NASDAQ has fared slightly better**, cushioned by continued earnings strength in large-cap technology — particularly companies with direct AI revenue exposure.

European indices have been more volatile. The DAX and FTSE 100 have whipsawed on energy price uncertainty, with Germany particularly exposed given its industrial base's dependence on stable energy inputs.

In Asia, Indian markets have shown **notable resilience**, with the NIFTY 50 holding near record levels driven by domestic consumption growth and a steady flow of foreign institutional investment into the country's expanding technology and infrastructure sectors.

Emerging market equities have been the most affected by the strong dollar, which makes dollar-denominated debt more expensive and tends to push capital back toward US-denominated assets.

## What Is Driving Volatility Right Now

**Oil prices** are the single biggest macro driver of market sentiment in Q1 2026. With Brent crude up over **22% since the Iran conflict began**, energy costs are feeding into inflation expectations, which in turn puts pressure on central bank decisions around interest rates.

The **US Federal Reserve** has held rates steady so far this year after its cuts in late 2025, but the language from the most recent meeting suggested the Fed is increasingly cautious about cutting further while energy-driven inflation remains elevated. Markets that had priced in two rate cuts by mid-2026 are now reassessing those expectations.

**AI-related spending** continues to be a major positive force, particularly for semiconductor stocks, cloud infrastructure providers, and enterprise software companies. Companies that can demonstrate real revenue from AI deployments — not just pilot programmes — are being rewarded by the market with premium valuations.

On the negative side, **consumer discretionary stocks** are under pressure. Higher fuel costs and persistent mortgage rates are squeezing household budgets in the US and UK, and retail sales data has been softer than expected in both markets.

## Sectors Worth Watching

Three sectors stand out as particularly interesting over the next six months.

**Energy** is the obvious one. Oil majors like Shell, ExxonMobil, and Saudi Aramco have seen significant price appreciation, but the real opportunity may be in **energy infrastructure** — pipelines, LNG terminals, storage facilities — which benefit from sustained high prices without the same volatility as upstream producers.

**Defence** has been one of the strongest performing sectors globally. Governments across NATO, the Middle East, and Asia Pacific are accelerating military spending, which flows directly to defence contractors. **BAE Systems, Lockheed Martin, and Rheinmetall** have all posted significant gains year to date.

**Healthcare and biotech** is being underrated in the current noise. Several major clinical trial readouts are due in Q2 2026, and the sector trades at historically modest valuations relative to its earnings growth potential.

## What Smart Investors Are Actually Doing

The most experienced portfolio managers are not making dramatic moves right now. They are, broadly speaking, doing three things.

First, **maintaining higher-than-average cash positions** — not because they are bearish, but because cash gives them the optionality to buy quality assets at better prices if volatility continues.

Second, **tilting toward quality over growth**. Companies with strong balance sheets, stable cash flows, and pricing power tend to outperform in uncertain environments. The era of "growth at any price" that defined the post-2020 market has firmly ended.

Third, **paying close attention to geopolitical timelines**. If the Iran situation de-escalates — or even just stabilises — energy prices would drop quickly, giving the Fed more room to cut rates, which would be a meaningful positive catalyst for equities broadly.

The lesson of the past quarter is one that long-term investors already knew: markets are not predictable in the short run, but they are remarkably consistent in the long run. Companies that create genuine value keep creating it. Trying to time the exact bottom or top is, statistically speaking, a losing game.

The best thing most investors can do right now is review their allocations, ensure they are not overexposed to any single risk factor — particularly energy or geopolitical risk — and resist the urge to react to every headline.

It has been exactly one month since the United States and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Iran on February 28, 2026. In that time, the conflict has evolved far beyond what many initial analysts predicted — and its ripple effects are now being felt across the entire Middle East, in global oil markets, and in diplomatic corridors from Washington to Beijing.

Here is a clear breakdown of what has happened, what is escalating, and what the world is watching closely right now.

## How We Got Here

The strikes on February 28 were the culmination of months of rising tension over Iran's nuclear programme and its continued support for proxy militias across the region. The US and Israel argued that diplomatic options had been exhausted. Iran's leadership, meanwhile, had shown no willingness to halt uranium enrichment beyond internationally agreed limits.

The initial strikes targeted **nuclear facilities, military command centres, and key missile production sites**. Within hours, Iran launched retaliatory missile and drone attacks on Israeli territory — most of which were intercepted — and threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly **20% of the world's oil supply** passes every day.

## Where Things Stand Today

One month in, the situation has become significantly more complicated than a two-party confrontation.

**An Iranian strike on a Saudi air base** wounded several US service members stationed there, pulling Saudi Arabia — which had been attempting to stay neutral — further into the conflict's orbit. Saudi Arabia has not officially entered the war, but its airspace and facilities are now clearly part of the operational theatre.

In Iraq, US and Iranian-backed militias have been clashing with increasing frequency. Senior analysts are now describing Iraq as an **"expanding battleground"** — a country that is being dragged into a war it did not start and cannot easily escape.

On the Yemeni front, Israel intercepted a missile launched from Houthi territory on Saturday, marking yet another front being activated in what is rapidly becoming a multi-theatre conflict.

- **Iran's nuclear programme**: Status of facilities remains disputed; satellite imagery suggests partial damage
- **Strait of Hormuz**: Remains open under President Trump's extended deadline
- **Oil prices**: **Brent crude has surged over 22%** since hostilities began
- **US military posture**: Carrier strike groups remain deployed in the region

## The Strait of Hormuz — The World's Most Watched Waterway

The single most consequential variable in this conflict is the Strait of Hormuz. Iran has threatened repeatedly to close it, which would choke off oil exports from Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, and Iran itself.

President Trump has extended his deadline for Iran to keep the strait open, but also warned explicitly that **Iran's energy infrastructure would be destroyed** if ships are prevented from passing. It is a high-stakes standoff, and both sides understand that closing the strait would cause an economic shock felt from Mumbai to Munich.

Global energy markets have been pricing in this risk. Fuel costs are rising in Europe and Asia, and several governments have begun drawing down strategic petroleum reserves as a precaution.

## International Reactions — Divided and Fragmented

The international response has been deeply fragmented. The United Nations Security Council has been unable to pass any binding resolution, with Russia and China blocking Western-backed proposals while the US has vetoed resolutions critical of the strikes.

Protests erupted in Rome and several European capitals this week, including a large anti-war demonstration in Britain. Within the United States, the "No Kings" movement — which has been marching against the Trump administration's domestic policies — has incorporated anti-war sentiment into its rallies.

China has called for an immediate ceasefire and offered to mediate talks, positioning itself as a neutral diplomatic actor. Whether Tehran would accept Chinese mediation remains unclear, but it is a signal of how actively Beijing is seeking to expand its global diplomatic role.

## What Comes Next

The coming weeks are likely to determine whether this conflict escalates into something far larger or settles into a prolonged, lower-intensity standoff. Several factors will be decisive.

**Iran's decision on the Strait of Hormuz** is the most immediate pressure point. If it closes, the economic consequences would force a broader international response. If it stays open, the conflict may remain contained — devastating for the countries directly involved, but manageable for the global economy.

The **diplomatic track** is also quietly alive. Trump's extension of the Hormuz deadline was widely read as leaving a door open for negotiations. Iran's leadership has signalled it is willing to talk, but only if the strikes stop first — a condition the US has not yet accepted.

What is certain is that the Middle East has entered a period of profound instability, and the outcome of this conflict will reshape alliances, energy markets, and the regional order for years to come.

The world is watching. And the next few weeks will tell us a great deal about where things are headed.
